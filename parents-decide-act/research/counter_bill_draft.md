# [DRAFT] THE DIGITAL LIBERTY AND PRIVACY ACT OF 2026

**To protect the First Amendment rights of all Americans, safeguard the privacy of families, and prohibit the mandatory collection of biometric and identifying data by operating system providers as a condition of digital participation.**

---

### SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.
This Act may be cited as the **“Digital Liberty and Privacy Act.”**

### SECTION 2. FINDINGS.
Congress finds the following:
1. The First Amendment protects the right of individuals to speak and associate anonymously.
2. Mandatory age verification at the operating system level constitutes a prior restraint on digital speech and assembly.
3. Forcing individuals to provide government-issued identification or sensitive personal data to a private corporation as a condition of using a general-purpose computing device creates a substantial burden on the exercise of constitutional liberties.
4. Families, not the government or big tech corporations, possess the fundamental right to direct the upbringing and digital exposure of their children.
5. The centralized storage of children’s identifying and biometric data by private corporations creates an attractive nuisance for bad actors and a permanent risk to the privacy and safety of minors.

### SECTION 3. PROHIBITION ON MANDATORY IDENTIFICATION FOR OPERATING SYSTEM ACCESS.
(a) **Prohibition.**—No operating system provider shall require a user to provide a government-issued identification, Social Security number, or biometric data as a mandatory condition for:
    (1) Initializing or setting up an account on a general-purpose computing device; or
    (2) Accessing the basic functional features of an operating system.

### SECTION 4. PROTECTION OF ANONYMITY AND PRIVATE PARENTAL OVERSIGHT.
(a) **Right to Anonymity.**—Users shall retain the right to utilize operating systems and general-purpose computing devices without being compelled to link such devices to a verified legal identity, except where strictly necessary for a specific commercial transaction initiated by the user.
(b) **Parental Primacy.**—Age verification and content filtering shall remain the voluntary prerogative of the parent or legal guardian. The government shall not mandate that private operating system providers act as de facto agents of the state in monitoring or verifying the familial relationships or ages of its citizens.

### SECTION 5. CRIMINAL PENALTIES FOR THE MANDATORY COLLECTION OF MINOR DATA.
(a) **Criminal Offense.**—It shall be a criminal offense for any corporation or operating system provider to knowingly and willfully require, collect, or store government-issued identification or biometric data from a minor under the age of 18 as a condition of accessing an operating system or general-purpose computing device.
(b) **Penalties.**—Any corporation found to have violated subsection (a) shall be subject to a fine of not less than $1,000,000 per violation. Any officer or director of a corporation who knowingly authorized such collection shall be subject to imprisonment for not more than 5 years.

### SECTION 6. REMOVAL OF CORPORATE PROTECTIONS AND IMMUNITY.
(a) **Forfeiture of Immunity.**—Any operating system provider or digital service provider that knowingly and willfully collects and stores identifying data of minors in violation of this Act shall forfeit all statutory immunities, including but not limited to protections under Section 230 of the Communications Decency Act (47 U.S.C. § 230).
(b) **Strict Liability and Personal Capacity.**—Corporations that store such data shall be held strictly liable for any damages resulting from a data breach, unauthorized access, or misuse of said data, regardless of the security measures in place at the time of the breach. Furthermore, the individual members, officers, directors, and shareholders of such corporations may be sued in their personal capacity and shall be held individually liable for damages arising from the unauthorized collection or storage of minor data, effectively piercing the corporate veil for any violation of this Act.

### SECTION 7. PREEMPTION OF MANDATORY VERIFICATION STATUTES.
This Act shall preempt any state or federal law that requires operating system providers to verify the age of users through mandatory data collection, where such laws fail to provide a non-identifying, privacy-preserving alternative that respects the user's right to anonymity.

### SECTION 8. ENFORCEMENT.
(a) **Private Right of Action.**—Any individual whose rights under this Act are violated by an operating system provider or a government entity may bring a civil action in an appropriate United States district court for declaratory and injunctive relief.
(b) **Attorney's Fees.**—In any action brought under this section, the court may award a prevailing plaintiff reasonable attorney's fees and costs.

### LEGAL COMMENTARY (ACLJ PERSPECTIVE)
*This counter-bill, the **Digital Liberty and Privacy Act**, is designed to strike at the heart of the unconstitutional overreach found in H.R. 8250. While H.R. 8250 purports to protect children, it does so by sacrificing the First Amendment rights of every American. By mandating age verification at the OS level, the government is effectively demanding a "digital passport" for the exercise of free speech. We cannot allow the government to outsource its surveillance to Big Tech. This draft restores parental primacy and protects the fundamental rights of families. Furthermore, we have introduced **criminal penalties** and **forfeiture of immunity** for any corporation that dares to treat our children’s data as a commodity or a condition of digital life. If Big Tech insists on building these databases of our children, they will do so at the risk of their own corporate existence, the personal liberty of their executives, and the personal assets of their shareholders.*
