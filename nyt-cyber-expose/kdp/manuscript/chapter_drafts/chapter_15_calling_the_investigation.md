# Chapter Fifteen: Calling the Investigation Determining the appropriate investigative authority for the Akhter breach requires weighing the scope tools and incentives of each potential overseer. These include the Office of Inspector General OIG the Government Accountability Office GAO congressional select committees and a special counsel. Each body possesses distinct competencies legal authorities and political dynamics that shape what they can compel and what they typically choose to pursue. Understanding these differences is essential to assessing why the breach was investigated the way it was and what reforms would ensure a more proportionate response to future architectural failures. First consider the agency Offices of Inspector General. OIGs are statutory entities embedded within each federal department tasked with detecting and preventing fraud waste abuse and mismanagement and with promoting economy efficiency and effectiveness in agency programs. They possess subpoena power can administer oaths and have access to federal law enforcement resources through partnerships with the DOJ and agency-specific investigative arms. An OIG-led investigation into the Akhter breach would likely focus on whether Opexus complied with its contractual security obligations whether the agency’s contracting officer’s representative adequately monitored performance and whether internal reporting mechanisms functioned as intended. OIGs are particularly adept at reviewing procurement compliance contract management and the flow of information between contractor and agency making them well-suited to examine the offboarding failure the credential-harvesting prelude and the whistleblower reporting gaps highlighted in Chapters 6 9 and 14. However OIG investigations are often constrained by their mandate to remain within the parent agency. They cannot easily compel cross-agency data sharing or issue binding directives that affect multiple departments simultaneously. If the breach revealed a systemic issue affecting dozens of agencies—as the Akhter case did given Opexus’s forty-five plus federal clients—an individual OIG might address only its own slice of the problem leaving the broader architectural vulnerability unaddressed. Second the Government Accountability Office. GAO is an independent nonpartisan agency that works for Congress auditing federal programs and expenditures evaluating the effectiveness of government policies and providing investigative and legal support to congressional committees. GAO’s strengths lie in its broad mandate its ability to issue binding recommendations though not enforce them directly and its capacity to conduct cross-cutting studies that span multiple agencies. A GAO investigation into the Akhter breach would likely examine the effectiveness of federal contractor oversight mechanisms the adequacy of background-check standards for IT roles the implementation of zero-trust architecture across the federal enterprise and the gap between recommended security controls and actual implementation in contractor-managed environments. GAO is particularly well-positioned to address the blob described in Chapter 10—the layered accumulation of legacy procurement decisions outdated architectures and diffused accountability that resists rapid remediation. Its high-risk list and biennial reports on federal IT management provide a ready framework for assessing whether the Akhter breach was an anomaly or a symptom of a wider pattern. However GAO lacks direct enforcement power. Its recommendations must be adopted by agencies and OMB to take effect and its investigations can take months or years to complete during which time the vulnerability may persist or be exploited again. Third congressional select committees. When a breach reaches a certain threshold of public interest or perceived national security impact Congress may establish a select committee or task an existing committee such as the House Oversight and Reform Committee the Senate Homeland Security and Governmental Affairs Committee or the intelligence committees to conduct a focused investigation. Congressional investigations offer the most potent investigative tools: they can compel testimony issue subpoenas hold public hearings and crucially enact legislation to close the gaps they uncover. A congressional inquiry into the Akhter breach would likely examine whether federal agencies are adequately overseeing contractor security practices whether the whistleblower protections extend to contractor employees in IT roles and what statutory or regulatory changes are needed to prevent recurrence. The political nature of congressional investigations means they are highly responsive to public pressure and media attention—making them more likely to pursue a high-profile case like the Akhter operation—but also subject to partisan dynamics that can affect the scope tone and ultimate recommendations of the report. Moreover congressional investigations are often time-limited. Once the committee issues its final report and any accompanying legislation its investigative authority lapses unless renewed. Fourth a special counsel or independent investigator. In rare cases involving alleged high-level misconduct potential obstruction or conflicts of interest that could compromise a standard agency or congressional investigation the Attorney General or a congressional leader may appoint a special counsel with broad investigative powers and a degree of independence from the usual chain of command. A special counsel investigation into the Akhter breach would likely focus on whether any senior officials attempted to impede the investigation whether there was evidence of collusion between the contractor and agency officials to conceal the breach and whether the breach revealed systemic corruption in the federal contracting process. While such an appointment would signal gravity and ensure insulation from political interference it is typically reserved for cases involving allegations of criminal conduct by senior officials—not for architectural failures rooted in incentive misalignment and legacy systems. The Akhter breach as documented stems more from systemic blind spots than from individual corruption making a special counsel less likely to be the first or most appropriate recourse. Given the nature of the Akhter breach—a systemic architectural failure enabled by incentive misalignment outdated monitoring and contractor-agency accountability gaps—the most appropriate investigative approach would likely be a hybrid: a GAO-led study to assess the cross-agency pattern and issue binding-style recommendations coupled with congressional oversight to ensure those recommendations are translated into enforceable statute or regulation. The OIGs of the affected agencies could conduct parallel agency-specific reviews to address localized compliance issues. This layered approach would allow for both the macro-level diagnosis of the blob and the micro-level correction of specific contractual failures while preserving the independence and analytical rigor needed to avoid capture by either the contractor lobby or the agency’s internal bureaucracy. To close this gap would require clarifying the division of labor among these investigative bodies in advance of any breach. One approach would be to establish a standing federal cybersecurity oversight board comprising representatives from GAO the OIG community CISA and congressional staff with a mandate to: receive and triage reports of major IT incidents assign investigative lead based on the incident’s scope and nature ensure timely sharing of findings and prevent duplicative efforts and track the implementation of recommendations across agencies and contractors. Another would be to amend the Inspectors General Act to explicitly authorize OIGs to issue cross-agency alerts and joint recommendations when an incident affects multiple departments thereby reducing the need for ad-hoc congressional interventions. A third would be to strengthen GAO’s enforcement arm by linking its recommendations to automatic OMB reviews or conditional grant funding thereby increasing the cost of non-compliance. None of these measures are free or simple. Each would require legislative action bureaucratic negotiation and a cultural shift in how federal oversight is conceptualized and resourced. But as the Akhter case demonstrates the cost of leaving the investigative architecture ambiguous and reactive is already being measured in deleted databases exfiltrated personal records and the lingering question of how many other breaches might go uninvestigated or inadequately addressed because the responsible party was unclear or the tools were mismatched to the problem.
