# Chapter Nine: The Contractor's Dashboard The view from Opexus's monitoring systems during the fifty-six-minute window on February 18 2025 is not fully captured in the public record. What we know comes from the Statement of Facts: the brothers’ timeline of termination first access and the initial destructive commands. What remains hidden—the internal dashboard alert thresholds and human judgment calls—must be reconstructed from documented contractor monitoring norms and the structural incentives that shape them. This reconstruction is labeled PARTIAL-COMPOSITE: the core facts (termination at 4:50–4:55 PM first connection at 4:56 PM first DROP DATABASE at 4:58 PM) are documentary. The internal NOC workflow detail is inferred from comparable FedRAMP-certified environments and contractor KPI structures. At approximately 4:50–4:55 p.m. the Microsoft Teams termination call ended. Muneeb and Sohaib Akhter were informed that their employment with Opexus was terminated effective immediately. The human-resources workflow—exit interview equipment return benefits counsel—proceeded as prescribed. The access revocation workflow did not trigger. According to the Statement of Facts Muneeb’s account remained active for nearly an hour after the call ended. This permitted him to open a connection to the Opexus-managed environment at 4:56 p.m. What Opexus's monitoring systems showed during the subsequent fifty-six minutes is not publicly disclosed. In a typical FedRAMP-authorized contractor environment the security information and event management SIEM platform aggregates logs from firewalls intrusion detection systems domain controllers and database servers. Analysts in the network operations center NOC review dashboards that display metrics such as login volume privileged command frequency and anomaly scores derived from behavioral baselines. The exact configuration of Opexus’s SIEM—including which data sources were ingested what correlation rules were active and how alerts were escalated—is not in the Statement of Facts or indictment. To approximate the internal view we turn to documented contractor monitoring norms. Most federal contractors that host multi-tenant platforms prioritize metrics that align with their service-level agreements: uptime percentage mean time to respond to tickets and number of resolved alerts. Security-focused metrics—such as atypical DROP DATABASE sequences large-volume data exports to removable media or log-clearing queries—are often either absent from the default dashboard or set to thresholds so high that they rarely fire. This is not an accident. It reflects the political economy of the contract. Contractors are paid to keep systems running and to close tickets quickly. Escalating a potential security incident opens a performance review consumes analyst time and risks breaching uptime guarantees if a false positive triggers unnecessary investigation. Under-flagging carries little to no cost unless a breach is later confirmed and traced back to a lapse in monitoring—a scenario that until February 2025 had not occurred in the public record for this contractor class. The termination-access coupling gap further illustrates the misalignment. Opexus later acknowledged in conversations with federal oversight agencies that additional diligence should have been applied to the offboarding process. Whether its SLA required automated account disabling upon termination and whether that requirement was contractually enforceable remains a FOIA question. In many federal contractor agreements offboarding is treated as a discrete HR function rather than an integrated security control. The HR system may emit a termination notice but unless that notice is programmatically fed into the identity-and-access-management IAM platform or unless the IAM platform polls HR on a short interval there is no automatic coupling. Even when such coupling exists the grace period often fifteen or thirty minutes can be insufficient if the attacker acts within the first minute—as the Akhters did. The structural verdict emerges from this reconstruction: the monitoring system did not fail to detect a breach. It detected an authorized session that should have been unauthorized—a distinction that requires human judgment contextual awareness and a willingness to escalate based on subtle anomalies. The workflow as built was not designed to apply that judgment automatically. Instead it relied on analysts to notice interpret and act on signals that in this case never rose above the noise floor because the operation mimicked normal maintenance activity: a single user issuing SQL commands over a fifty-six-minute window a duration that fits within standard batch-job expectations. When the brothers fled the scene—Sohaib to his home Muneeb initially to his apartment before driving to Texas with his laptop phone and PIV card—they left behind a network humming with silent failures. Logging existed but alerting did not. Monitoring existed but response did not. The contractor state had built a dashboard that displayed everything except the most important metric: whether the people with access were still supposed to have it.
