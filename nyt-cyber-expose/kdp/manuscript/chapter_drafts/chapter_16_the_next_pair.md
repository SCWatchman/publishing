# Chapter Sixteen: The Next Pair The Akhter brothers’ capture and conviction do not eliminate the vulnerability they exposed. Every federal agency that relied on Opexus software—forty-five plus clients spanning the Department of Homeland Security the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission the Department of Veterans Affairs the Small Business Administration and others—ran the same infrastructure that failed to revoke terminated employees’ access in real time. The structural flaw was not confined to a single contract or a single bad actor. It was a feature of the contractor state itself reproduced across dozens of agency networks that shared the same architectural assumptions about trust monitoring and accountability. To see how deeply this pattern is embedded one need only look at a case that predates the Akhters by nearly a decade: the Awan brothers. Imran Abid and Jamal Awan along with associates such as Hina Alvi and Rao Abbas held information technology positions within the U.S. House of Representatives from approximately 2004 to 2017—over thirteen years of continuous access to the congressional network. In February 2017 they were suddenly barred from the House network amid suspicions of unauthorized server access procurement irregularities and possible data exfiltration. Subsequent investigations led to a series of legal outcomes: Imran Awan pleaded guilty to making a false statement on a loan application unrelated to the House IT work received probation and served no additional prison time. His wife Hina Alvi fled to Pakistan and remains under investigation. The civil lawsuit filed by the brothers against members of Congress for wrongful termination resulted in a settlement of approximately $850000 in 2020. Notably no charges were ever brought related to the alleged unauthorized access or data exfiltration from the House network—a fact that underscores the difficulty of proving insider threat when logs are inconclusive attribution is murky and the political will to pursue the case wanes. Yet the structural parallels between the Awan and Akhter cases are striking despite the decade that separates them. In both instances the individuals involved held privileged access to sensitive systems through contractor or contractor-adjacent arrangements. In both cases background checks failed to capture prior convictions or red-flag behavior: the Awans’ prior allegations of misconduct were not discovered in the vetting process just as the Akhters’ 2015 felony pleas fell outside the typical seven-year lookback window used by contractors like Opexus. In both cases the scope of access granted far exceeded what was necessary for the stated job function—the Awans reportedly had broad administrative privileges on the House network while the Akhters could issue arbitrary SQL commands against dozens of federal agency databases hosted on a shared platform. And in both cases the monitoring and alerting systems in place did not flag the anomalous behavior in real time allowing the actors to operate for extended periods before any meaningful intervention occurred. This raises the scaling problem central to this chapter: the Akhter case is documented with a plea agreement a Statement of Facts and a superseding indictment that provide a granular footnote-able account of what happened. The Awan case is documented though to a lesser extent through court filings congressional reports and a civil settlement. The question this chapter poses is simple but chilling: how many undocumented cases share the same architecture? How many other contractor-managed platforms host multiple federal agencies’ data rely on background checks that miss older convictions and lack real-time behavioral analytics for privileged-access abuse? The answer implied by the persistence of the findings in GAO high-risk lists and OIG audit reports is likely many. The blob described in Chapter Ten is not a singular anomaly. It is a systemic feature of federal IT outsourcing that creates repeated opportunities for insiders with legitimate credentials to act—whether for destruction exfiltration or persistence—before the architecture catches up. Deterrence theory offers a lens through which to view why these patterns persist despite the severe penalties on the table. Muneeb Akhter faces up to forty-five years in prison if sentenced consecutively on all counts. Sohaib Akhter convicted on May 7 2026 faces up to twenty-one years. These are not trivial exposures. Yet the brothers proceeded anyway—suggesting that in their calculation the expected value of the operation in terms of disruption capability demonstration psychological satisfaction or potential leverage from the exfiltrated data exceeded the expected cost even when adjusted for the high probability of eventual attribution and conviction. The fact that they discussed blackmail and dismissed it on evidentiary—not moral—grounds indicates that they were not deterred by the prospect of prison. They were deterred by the prospect of making the prosecution’s job too easy. If the next actor values anonymity the long sentence may deter. But if the next actor has already calculated that attribution is unlikely or that the value of being known outweighs the cost of incarceration then the calculus flips and the architecture’s invitation remains open. The structural verdict is unavoidable: the architecture invites replication. It does so by externalizing liability diffusing accountability and structuring incentives to prioritize uptime over security. It does so by relying on background checks that tire out after seven years by decoupling termination from access revocation and by monitoring systems that are blind to low-volume high-signal anomalies unless explicitly tuned. The question then is not whether another Akhter or another Awan will appear—it is what closes the invitation before it is accepted again. Possible answers include: mandating zero-trust architecture so that no credential contractor or otherwise grants blanket access to multiple agency datasets implementing behavioral anomaly monitoring that flags deviations such as a contractor engineer issuing dozens of DROP DATABASE statements or exporting large result sets to removable media reforming contractor accountability under the Federal Acquisition Regulation to treat security-related SLAs with the same financial weight as uptime guarantees and extending whistleblower protections to contractor employees in federal IT security roles so that those who see warning signs have a safe path to report them. None of these measures are free or simple. Each would require legislative action budgetary shifts and a cultural change in how federal IT is valued and managed. But as the Akhter case demonstrates the cost of allowing the architecture to persist is already being measured in deleted databases exfiltrated personal records and the lingering question of how many other actors are already walking through the open door—whether they know it yet or not.
