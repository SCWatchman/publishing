# Chapter Eight: Covering the Burn The Akhter brothers did not limit themselves to deletion and exfiltration. They engaged in a deliberate cover-up that began while the databases were still falling and continued for months afterward. Every beat of this effort comes directly from the Statement of Facts attached to Muneeb Akhter’s April 2026 plea agreement and the Superseding Indictment. These documents together document the brothers’ attempts to erase evidence of their conduct and preserve access for future exploitation. At 459 p.m.—while databases were still being dropped—Muneeb Akhter opened an AI tool and typed two queries that would become central to the cover-up narrative. He asked: “How do i clear system logs from SQL servers after deleting databases?” Moments later he asked: “How do you clear all event and application logs from Microsoft windows server 2012?” The tool provided answers. The brothers followed them. This exchange is not speculative. It appears verbatim in the Statement of Facts. This demonstrates that the cover-up was not an afterthought but an integral part of the operation’s execution phase. Sohaib Akhter observed Muneeb “cleaning out their database backups” in real time according to the Statement of Facts. While the exact method is not specified in public documents the observation confirms that the brothers targeted not only the live databases but also the backup repositories that agencies relied on for restoration. By attacking both primary and secondary storage they sought to widen the restoration gap and increase the likelihood that at least some of the deleted data would be permanently lost. On February 21–22 2025—a weekend shortly after the operation—a co-conspirator identity not specified in the public record reinstalled the operating systems on both company-issued laptops used by the Akhters. This overwrote local evidence that might have survived the initial deletion and log-clearing steps. This act though not directly attributed to either brother in the Statement of Facts is consistent with their demonstrated willingness to involve trusted associates in post-operation cleanup. It suggests a division of labor that extended beyond the fifty-six-minute window. What survived these efforts however proved sufficient for investigators to reconstruct the breach. The AI query logs themselves—records of the brothers’ questions and the tool’s responses—remained in the system. They provided a timestamped trail of intent. The USB drive recovered during the December 3 2025 arrest contained the 1805 EEOC complaint files and 450 taxpayer records that had been staged for exfiltration. It also contained operational metadata such as database schemas and API endpoints. Most critically Muneeb retained his Personal Identity Verification (PIV) card—a high-security smart card that grants physical access to federal facilities and logical access to certain systems—when he left the premises on February 18. That card would later be used in a March 2025 attempt to regain entry to federal buildings and in a Texas-based credential-stuffing campaign that ran until his arrest. The brothers’ cover-up highlights a critical limitation in the contractor state’s monitoring and incident response architecture. Organizations often collect logs and maintain backup systems. Yet they frequently lack the correlation and retention policies needed to detect low-volume high-impact insider activity. Log clearing can remove evidence of command execution. But if the logging system itself is not tamper-proof or if logs are not forwarded to a secure immutable repository a determined insider can erase their tracks at the source. Similarly backups are only effective if they are isolated from the production environment and tested regularly. If an attacker has access to both primary and backup storage the restoration guarantee evaporates. To close this gap would require rethinking how federal contractors handle privileged access monitoring and data protection. One approach would be to implement immutable append-only audit logs. Examples include write-once-read-many WORM storage or cryptographically signed logs that cannot be altered or deleted by users with privileged access. Another would be to enforce separation of duties. The individuals who run `DROP DATABASE` commands should not also have the ability to modify or delete the logs that record those commands. A third would be to maintain air-gapped encrypted backups of critical databases with restoration drills conducted quarterly to ensure viability. Finally contractors should deploy file integrity monitoring on critical system binaries and configuration files to detect unauthorized changes such as OS reinstalls or driver tampering. None of these measures are free or simple. Each would add cost latency and operational complexity. But as the Akhter case demonstrates the cost of assuming that logging and backups alone provide sufficient protection is already being measured in deleted databases exfiltrated personal records and the lingering question of how many other contractors rely on the same fragile assumptions.
