# Chapter Six: Disguise as Protocol On February 1 2025 two weeks before his termination Muneeb Akhter sent a plaintext message to his twin brother Sohaib via Microsoft Teams. He asked: “Can you get me the plaintext password for [EEOC complainant]?” Sohaib still possessing elevated database access as an Opexus engineering employee conducted a query on the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission’s public portal database. He returned the credentials. That password would later be used to access the complainant’s personal email without authorization—a detail confirmed in the brothers’ subsequent guilty pleas and the Statement of Facts. This exchange was not an isolated anomaly. It reflected a pattern of credential sharing that had become routine within the Opexus engineering team—a tacit acceptance of security shortcuts in the name of operational convenience. The critical failure came on February 18 2025 at approximately 4:50 p.m. during a remote termination meeting conducted over Microsoft Teams. Present were Muneeb Akhter his supervisor and an HR representative. Sohaib Akhter was not in attendance though he remained employed at that moment. The meeting concluded at approximately 4:55 p.m. after which Muneeb’s access should have been revoked per standard offboarding procedures. Instead his account remained active. At 4:56 p.m. just one minute after the meeting’s end Muneeb used those still-valid credentials to log into the Company-1 environment. This environment is a FedRAMP-certified platform hosting case management and FOIA response software for over forty-five federal agencies. The window was not hypothetical. Federal contracting rules and Opexus’s own service-level agreement required immediate deactivation upon termination. This is typically defined as within fifteen minutes of separation. Yet no automated trigger fired. No alert sounded. And no human intervened. The account remained active. This delay was not a mere oversight. It was a structural feature of the contractor state. Offboarding was treated as a discrete manual process rather than an integrated security function. Contractors like Opexus billed for hours worked. They did not bill for the speed of access revocation. Incentives aligned with continuity. They did not align with containment. The consequences were immediate. Within seconds of login Muneeb began enumerating accessible systems. By 4:58 p.m. he had issued his first destructive command. The offboarding failure was not a gap in procedure. It was the procedure itself. This procedure was designed for convenience. It was not designed for security. When Sohaib learned of his brother’s actions later that evening he did not report them. Instead he participated. Over the following ten months the twins would use those same EEOC credentials and others harvested from Opexus systems to conduct a credential-stuffing campaign that compromised hundreds of personal accounts across government and private sectors. The blindness was not in missing a single step. It was in believing that the steps as performed were sufficient. The contractor state had outsourced operations. It had outsourced the very architecture of accountability. It had forgotten to ask who would watch the watchers.
