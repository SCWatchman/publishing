# Chapter Eleven: The Contractor's Dashboard At approximately 5:52 p.m. on February 18 2025 after fifty-six minutes of sustained database deletion and data exfiltration Muneeb Akhter’s rampage slowed. The command prompt blinked awaiting input. Around him the digital wreckage mounted: ninety-six U.S. government databases marked for deletion 1,805 EEOC complaint files copied to a USB drive 450 taxpayer records exfiltrated and his Personal Identity Verification PIV card still in his possession—a physical token that retained access to federal facilities and systems. The scale of the deletion was not random. Court records show the brothers targeted systems supporting case management Freedom of Information Act processing and other administrative functions across at least twelve federal agencies including the Department of Homeland Security the Department of Veterans Affairs and the Small Business Administration. Yet the most telling detail was not what they destroyed but what they retained: the PIV card. Issued by Opexus for facility access the card remained active and would later grant Muneeb renewed entry to federal buildings—including a March 2025 visit to a firearms dealer in Farnham Virginia where he assisted Sohaib in attempting to sell seven weapons recovered from a search warrant at Sohaib’s Alexandria residence. The USB drive recovered during the December 3 2025 arrest contained not only the EEOC files but also a trove of operational metadata: database schemas user lists and API endpoints—information that could be used to re-establish access or launch further attacks. The brothers’ actions revealed a critical gap in the contractor state’s monitoring architecture: while Opexus logged internal system access it lacked real-time anomaly detection for bulk data exfiltration or unauthorized database deletion commands. No alert fired when `DROP DATABASE` statements cascaded across multiple schemas. No dashboard flashed red as gigabytes of EEOC case files were copied to removable media. This blindness was structural. Contractors like Opexus were compensated for uptime and ticket resolution not for the speed of intrusion detection. Their security information and event management SIEM tools if present were tuned to noise levels acceptable in a shared multi-tenant environment—not to the subtle signals of an insider with legitimate credentials executing a pre-planned destruction sequence. When the brothers fled the scene—Sohaib to his home Muneeb initially to his apartment before driving to Texas with his laptop phone and PIV card—they left behind a network humming with silent failures. Logging existed but alerting did not. Monitoring existed but response did not. The contractor state had built a dashboard that displayed everything except the most important metric: whether the people with access were still supposed to have it. In the weeks that followed the FBI’s electronic surveillance would catch Muneeb attempting to log into government systems from a Texas hotel room using credentials harvested from Opexus—a final reminder that access once granted is not easily rescinded in a system built on trust rather than verification. The contractor's dashboard showed green lights across the board. Meanwhile the breach had already happened.
